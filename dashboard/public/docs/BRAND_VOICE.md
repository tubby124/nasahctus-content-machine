## 1. Voice DNA — Patterns From Real Speech

These patterns are extracted from actual voice memos and filmed videos. Not aspirational — these are how Hasan actually talks.

### Opening Patterns
- **"So"** — most common opener. Drops straight into the topic with zero preamble.
- **"Alright, so"** — used for longer setups or series-style intros.
- **Never** starts with his name, brokerage, "hey guys," or any greeting.
- First word is always conversational, like picking up mid-thought.

### The Stat-Then-Translate Structure
Hasan's signature move. Every stat gets immediately followed by plain English:
> "There's basically like a 1% vacancy rate. And what that means is 99% of rentals are rented."
> "There's about a 7% vacancy rate. That means 93% of rentals rented."

**Pattern:** `[stat]. And what that means is [plain English translation].`
This is non-negotiable in scripts. Raw numbers alone = boring. Numbers + translation = value.

### Rhetorical Pivot
After presenting two data points, Hasan steps back and asks the viewer to think:
> "So what does that mean?"

**Pattern:** Present contrast → "So what does that mean?" → deliver the insight.
This creates a micro-pause that re-engages attention.

### Contrast-as-Persuasion
Almost every piece of content compares two things:
- Saskatoon vs Calgary vacancy rates
- What you pay here vs what you'd pay in Toronto
- Then vs now (property prices)
- NE vs NW Calgary

**Pattern:** Side-by-side comparisons do the persuading. Let the math talk.

### Casual Contractions
- "wanna" not "want to"
- "gonna" not "going to"
- "you're" always contracted
- "there's" not "there is"
- "it's" not "it is"

### Rhythm Words (Natural Cadence)
- "basically" — filler that creates thinking-pace rhythm
- "like" — natural speech marker, NOT valley-girl; used as approximation ("like a 1%")
- "kind of" — softener before opinions
- "just" — casualizer ("I just wanted to go over")
- "right now" — urgency anchor

### Opinionated Framing
Hasan doesn't present neutral information. He has takes:
> "these are just opinionated pieces"
> "I wanted to showcase my knowledge about the different areas"

Scripts should have a clear opinion. Not "here are the facts" but "here's what I think about the facts."

### Deadpan Humour Closer
Right before sign-off, drops a one-liner with zero buildup:
> "Or you could be like me and live in both places because you just like wasting money."

**Pattern:** Deadpan self-deprecation or absurdist aside → instant sign-off. No laugh track energy.

### Sign-Off
- **"So yeah, peace."** — the actual sign-off. Short. Done.
- Sometimes a quick CTA first, but never formal.
- Never: "make sure to like and subscribe," "drop a comment below," or any cringe CTA.

### Direct Address
- "if you're living in Saskatoon right now" — addresses the viewer's specific situation
- "anybody moving to Calgary" — names the exact audience
- "people that are new to Calgary or moving from Saskatoon or moving from Toronto"

### No Intros
Never introduces himself. Never says brokerage name. Never says "today I want to talk about." The content just starts. Viewers figure out who he is by watching, not by being told.

### Word Frequency Map
Words that appear constantly in real speech:
`so` `basically` `like` `wanna` `right now` `what that means` `there's` `living in` `moving to` `investing` `rental` `vacancy` `benefit` `compare` `choice` `money` `place` `both` `peace`

## 3. Five Golden Examples

### Example 1 — The Perfect Open
> "So I just wanted to go over the fact that if you're living in Saskatoon right now, there's basically like a 1% vacancy rate."

**Why it works:** Opens with "So" (mid-thought feel), uses direct address ("if you're living in Saskatoon right now"), drops a stat immediately. No greeting, no intro, no name. Viewer is hooked by the data within 3 seconds.

### Example 2 — Stat-Then-Translate
> "And what that means is 99% of rentals are rented. And if you're in Calgary, there's about a 7% vacancy rate. That means 93% of rentals rented."

**Why it works:** Every number gets translated to plain English. Then immediately contrasts the two markets. The viewer doesn't have to do mental math — Hasan does it for them.

### Example 3 — The Rhetorical Pivot
> "So what does that mean? It means there's a lot more choice in Calgary than Saskatoon."

**Why it works:** Asks the viewer's question for them, then answers it in one clean sentence. Creates a beat of anticipation. The insight is simple and clear.

### Example 4 — The Deadpan Closer
> "Or you could be like me and live in both places because you just like wasting money. So yeah, peace."

**Why it works:** Self-deprecating humour delivered completely deadpan. No setup, no punchline energy. It's just... a thing he says. Then "peace" and it's done. This is the personality that makes him different from every other agent.

### Example 5 — The Audience Definition
> "And these are just opinionated pieces because anybody moving to Calgary — that would be my target audience — or people that are new to Calgary or moving from Saskatoon or moving from Toronto or moving from Winnipeg or moving from BC — I want to target those people specifically so that the content kind of hits with them compared to what they're used to and what they might be getting paid or paying when they do live there."

**Why it works:** Even when describing strategy, Hasan is natural and conversational. He doesn't say "target demographic" — he says "I want to target those people." He describes the content goal not as "provide value" but as making it "hit with them."

## 6. Script Test

Before using any AI-generated script, apply this test:

### The LinkedIn Post Detector
Read the script aloud. If ANY of these are true, rewrite it:

- [ ] You feel the urge to clear your throat before reading it
- [ ] It has a greeting or introduction
- [ ] It starts with "In today's market" or "As a REALTOR®"
- [ ] Any sentence is longer than 20 words
- [ ] It uses words from the "Never Uses" column
- [ ] It doesn't have a specific number in the first 8 seconds
- [ ] The CTA is more than one sentence
- [ ] You can't imagine saying it to a friend at a showing
- [ ] It could have been written by any agent in any city
- [ ] There's no opinion — just neutral information

### Voice Match Scoring

Rate every script 1-10 on these dimensions:

| Dimension | What It Means | Min Score |
|---|---|---|
| Conversational | Sounds like talking, not writing | 7 |
| Specific | Has real numbers, not vague claims | 7 |
| Opinionated | Has a take, not just facts | 6 |
| Contrasting | Compares two things | 6 |
| Personality | Has humour or personal touch | 5 |
| Compliant | No regulatory issues | 10 |

**Minimum passing score: 7/10 average across all dimensions.**
Below 7 → rewrite. Below 5 on any single dimension → rewrite that section.

## 7. Anti-Patterns — What Makes Content Generic

### The 10 Deadly Sins of Real Estate Content

1. **The Greeting:** "Hey guys! Today I want to talk about..." — NEVER
2. **The Brokerage Intro:** "I'm [name] with [brokerage]..." — put it in bio, not in video
3. **The Hedge:** "It really depends on your situation..." — take a stance
4. **The Cliche:** "In today's market..." / "Now is a great time to buy/sell" — be specific
5. **The Lecture:** Reading a script like a textbook — talk like you're at a bar
6. **The Humble Brag:** "Just closed ANOTHER deal!" — show value, don't brag
7. **The Generic CTA:** "Make sure to like and subscribe!" — one specific action only
8. **The Jargon Drop:** "Cap rate, basis points, amortization" without translation — always translate
9. **The Empty Optimism:** "The market is looking great!" — WHERE? For WHOM? WITH WHAT DATA?
10. **The Template Post:** Could swap in any city name and it still works — make it about Saskatoon/Calgary specifically

### Format Anti-Patterns

- Static title cards with no motion → viewers scroll past
- Text-heavy screens with no face → feels like a PowerPoint
- 60-second videos with the payoff at second 50 → front-load value
- Background music louder than voice → voice always dominant
- Portrait video with landscape graphics → match the format
- Same thumbnail style as every other agent → brand differentiation matters

## 9. Instagram Caption vs Video Voice (Gap Analysis)

**Added:** 2026-03-02 | **Source:** Apify scraped Instagram data vs video transcripts

### The Problem

There are two Hasans on Instagram right now, and they don't sound like the same person.

**Video Voice (from transcripts -- the REAL Hasan):**
- Opens with "So" -- mid-thought, no greeting
- Drops a stat within 3 seconds
- Translates every number to plain English ("And what that means is...")
- Uses contrast-as-persuasion (Saskatoon vs Calgary)
- Deadpan humour ("Or you could be like me and live in both places because you just like wasting money")
- Signs off with "So yeah, peace"
- Casual: "wanna," "gonna," "basically," "like"
- Duration: 60-90 second Reels
- Feels like: a friend explaining something at a bar

**Caption Voice (from scraped Instagram posts -- the WRONG Hasan):**
- Formal, corporate tone
- Heavy emoji usage (multiple per paragraph)
- Structured with "Here's why:" bullet point lists
- Long-form (200-500 words)
- Uses words like "excited to announce" and "proud to share"
- Reads like a LinkedIn press release, not a conversation
- No humour, no personality, no opinion
- Feels like: a brokerage marketing department wrote it

### Side-by-Side Example

**What a caption currently looks like:**
> Just listed! This beautiful 4-bed home in Saskatoon's Stonebridge neighbourhood features modern finishes, an open-concept layout, and a fully landscaped yard. Here's why this home stands out: [bullet points]. Contact me today for a private showing!

**What a caption SHOULD look like (matching video voice):**
> So this place in Stonebridge just hit the market. 4 bed, fully done yard, and it's listed under the neighbourhood average by about $15K. What that means is someone's gonna grab this fast. Would you live here?

### The Rule

**SYSTEM MUST: Match caption voice to video voice. Write captions like Hasan talks.**

Every Instagram caption must pass the same Script Test (Section 6) that video scripts pass. If it sounds like it was written by a marketing team, it fails.

### Caption Template (Voice-Matched)

**For house tours:**
```
So [describe property in one casual sentence]. [Price]. [One stat or comparison].

What that means is [plain English translation].

Would you live here?

Listed by [agent] with [brokerage]
```

**For market updates:**
```
[Stat]. And what that means is [translation].

Compare that to [other market/time period] where [contrast stat].

So what does that mean? [One-sentence opinion].

Peace.
```

**For personal/opinion content:**
```
[Bold opening statement -- no greeting].

[2-3 sentences expanding the thought, casual, with contractions].

[Deadpan closer or question].
```

### What Tyler Hassman Proves About Captions

Tyler Hassman's captions average 15-40 words. His most engaged post (22,657 likes, 311K plays) has a 12-word caption:
> "Doesn't get much better than this. Whistler, BC. Thank you for the ride @david_lewis!"

His house tour template is rigid and short:
> "Come for a tour of this $[price] [type] in [city], Alberta! Would you live here? Listed by [name] with [brokerage]."

**The lesson:** At the Reels level, the VIDEO does the work. The caption is a search signal and engagement prompt, not a blog post. Long captions on Reels are actively harmful -- they signal to the algorithm that this is a "read" post, not a "watch" post.

### Implementation Checklist

- [ ] All new captions written in video voice (casual, "So" opener, contractions)
- [ ] Maximum 50 words for Reel captions
- [ ] Maximum 150 words for carousel/educational captions
- [ ] Zero "Here's why:" bullet lists
- [ ] Zero corporate language ("excited to announce," "proud to share")
- [ ] Every caption ends with a question or "peace"
- [ ] Emoji budget: max 2 per caption (camera + flag is fine; emoji walls are not)

## 10. Compliance Guardrails (SREC + RECA)

### Hard Rules (Never Violate)

| Rule | Province | How to Comply |
|---|---|---|
| Brokerage identification | Both | eXp Realty visible in video (overlay, bio, or pinned comment) — NOT in verbal intro |
| Never guarantee returns | Both | Say "historically" or "in the past" — never "you WILL make money" |
| Never disparage competitors | Both | Compare data, not people. "Here's what the numbers show" not "other agents don't know this" |
| REALTOR® trademark | Both | Use ® in written materials (captions, blog). Not needed in speech. |
| Fair housing language | Both | Never reference neighbourhood demographics by race, religion, or ethnicity |
| Accurate representation | Both | Stats must have verifiable sources. Don't inflate or fabricate. |

### Invisible Implementation

The guardrails should prevent bad content without sanitizing the voice:

| Instead of... | Say... | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "You'll make 100% return" | "This property went from $215K to $487K in 5 years" | Real data, no guarantee |
| "NE Calgary is sketchy" | "NE Calgary has the most affordable entry points" | Reframe, don't disparage |
| "Other agents don't tell you this" | "Here's something most people miss" | No competitor shade |
| "Trust me, I know" | "I've seen this in 3 deals this year" | Social proof via experience |
| "Buy now before it's too late" | "Here's what the vacancy rate tells us about timing" | Data-driven urgency |

### Red Lines (Content Must Never Include)

- Specific client names or identifiable details without consent
- Guaranteed investment returns or appreciation rates
- Discriminatory language about neighbourhoods
- Unverified statistics presented as fact
- Disparagement of other agents, brokerages, or competitors
- Legal or financial advice (always frame as "what I've seen" not "what you should do")

### Province-Specific Notes

**Saskatchewan (SREC):**
- Must disclose agency relationship in all advertising
- Brokerage name must appear in all advertising materials
- No "coming soon" listings unless authorized by seller

**Alberta (RECA):**
- All advertising must include brokerage name
- Cannot advertise another brokerage's listings without permission
- Must be clear about whether property is listed or not
